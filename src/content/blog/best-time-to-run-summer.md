In summer, *when* you run matters as much as *how far*. The difference between a 6 AM run and a noon run can be 20°F and the gap between a great workout and a dangerous one. But temperature isn't the only factor.

## The Obvious Answer: Early Morning

For most summer locations, the coolest hour is just before sunrise — typically 5–6 AM. Temperatures are at their daily minimum, humidity may be high but heat stress is lowest, and UV index is zero.

But "coolest" isn't always "best." Here's what else matters:

## The Five Factors of a Good Running Window

### 1. Temperature (Weight: High)

The ideal running temperature is **45–55°F**. Every degree above 55°F adds a penalty, and heat is weighted more heavily than cold because heat degrades performance faster.

In summer, this means mornings win. A 6 AM reading of 72°F is dramatically better than a 2 PM reading of 92°F.

### 2. Humidity / Dew Point (Weight: High in Summer)

When temperature exceeds 60°F, dew point becomes critical. The combined temp + dew point value drives pace adjustment:

- 6 AM: 72°F + 68°F dew point = 140 (hot, but manageable)
- 2 PM: 92°F + 72°F dew point = 164 (dangerous)

Dew point doesn't vary as much throughout the day as temperature, so the morning advantage is mostly about cooler air temperature.

### 3. UV Index (Weight: Moderate)

UV peaks between 10 AM and 4 PM, reaching 8–11+ in summer. Running under intense UV means:
- Higher skin temperature (adds to heat stress)
- Sunburn risk on exposed skin
- Need for sunscreen, hat, sunglasses

Early morning and evening runs avoid peak UV entirely. UV below 3 requires no special precautions.

### 4. Precipitation Probability (Weight: Very High)

Nobody wants to get caught in a thunderstorm. Summer afternoon thunderstorms are common in many regions. A 70% precipitation chance heavily penalizes that time slot regardless of temperature.

### 5. Daylight (Weight: Moderate)

Running in darkness adds safety concerns — you need reflective gear and a headlamp, and you're more vulnerable to trip hazards and traffic. A 15-point penalty for darkness makes daylight hours more attractive, but it doesn't override a massive temperature advantage.

## Summer Scenarios

### Brooklyn, July

- **5 AM:** 76°F, dark, calm → Score: ~35 (very good, but dark)
- **6 AM:** 78°F, sunrise, calm → Score: ~28 (**best**)
- **12 PM:** 92°F, UV 10, gusty → Score: ~95 (terrible)
- **8 PM:** 85°F, no UV, humid → Score: ~55 (OK but sticky)

**Best window: 6–7 AM.** Light breeze, low UV, tolerable heat.

### Phoenix, August

- **5 AM:** 88°F, dark, calm → Score: ~50
- **6 AM:** 90°F, sunrise → Score: ~55
- **4 PM:** 110°F, UV 9 → Score: ~150 (extreme)
- **8 PM:** 102°F, setting sun → Score: ~85

**Best window: 5 AM** — even before sunrise, it's still 88°F in Phoenix. There are no good options in August; 5 AM is just the least bad.

## The Should I Run Best Window Algorithm

[Should I Run](/) scores every hour of the day using a weighted penalty system:

- Temperature deviation from ideal (45–55°F)
- Humidity load (temp + dew point above 100)
- Wind above 10 mph
- Precipitation probability
- UV above 6
- Darkness penalty
- Ice risk (winter)

It then scans for the best 1-hour window and ranks the top three options — Best, Good, and Fair — so you can pick the slot that fits your schedule.

The algorithm naturally adapts to seasons: in summer it favors early mornings; in winter it favors midday warmth and daylight.

## Practical Tips

1. **Lay out your clothes the night before** if you're targeting an early morning slot. Reducing friction makes 5:30 AM more likely to happen.
2. **Pre-hydrate the evening before.** Drinking water at 5 AM doesn't help mid-run — you need to start hydrated.
3. **If mornings don't work, go post-sunset.** UV is gone, temperature is dropping, and you get a decent window before it's truly dark.
4. **Don't skip the run entirely.** A slow, shortened run in heat is better than no run. Consistency beats optimization.
