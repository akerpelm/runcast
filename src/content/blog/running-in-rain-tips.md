Rain doesn't have to cancel your run. In fact, some of the best runs happen in a light drizzle — cool air, empty paths, and a feeling of toughness that carries into the rest of your day. But there's a line between a character-building rain run and a dangerous one.

## When Rain Is Fine

**Light to moderate rain (< 0.1 in/hr)** with temperatures above 45°F is perfectly safe for most runners. You'll get wet, but your body generates enough heat to stay warm. The main concerns are:

- **Visibility** — cars may not see you as well
- **Footing** — painted road markings, metal grates, and leaves become slippery
- **Chafing** — wet skin + friction = trouble on longer runs

These are manageable with the right gear and route choices.

## When to Skip It

Move your run inside (or postpone) when:

- **Thunderstorms are forecast.** Lightning is the non-negotiable. If there's thunder, you should not be outside. Should I Run flags thunderstorm risk as a danger-level alert.
- **Freezing rain or sleet.** Any precipitation below 34°F creates ice on contact. This is Should I Run's highest ice risk scenario.
- **Heavy rain (> 0.3 in/hr)** combined with wind. Driving rain reduces visibility to near-zero and hypothermia risk increases rapidly below 50°F.
- **Flash flood warnings.** Avoid low-lying trails and paths near waterways.

## What to Wear in Rain

The general rule: **don't overdress**. A fully waterproof jacket traps heat and moisture from sweat, which can make you wetter *inside* the jacket than the rain would make you *outside* it.

### Above 55°F (Warm Rain)

- **Bottom:** Shorts (they dry fast; tights trap water)
- **Top:** T-shirt or singlet — embrace getting wet. A light rain shell if the rain is heavy, but you'll likely overheat in it.
- **Extras:** Brimmed cap (keeps rain off your face), body glide on high-friction areas

### 40–55°F (Cool Rain)

- **Bottom:** Shorts or half tights
- **Top:** Long sleeve + light water-resistant shell. Choose a jacket with ventilation — pit zips or mesh backing.
- **Extras:** Brimmed cap, lightweight gloves (wet hands get cold fast)

### Below 40°F (Cold Rain)

This is the highest-risk scenario. Wet + cold = hypothermia pathway.

- **Bottom:** Running tights (not cotton)
- **Top:** Base layer + water-resistant jacket (prioritize wind/waterproofing over breathability here)
- **Extras:** Waterproof gloves, brimmed cap, neck gaiter. Minimize exposed skin.
- **Route:** Stay close to home or have a bail-out option

## Shoe Considerations

- **Trail shoes** with lugged soles provide better grip on wet surfaces
- **Avoid cotton socks** — merino wool or synthetic blends prevent blisters when wet
- **Have a rotation** — don't run in soaked shoes the next day. Stuff them with newspaper to speed drying.

## The 50% Rule

Should I Run adds a "light rain shell" recommendation when precipitation probability hits 50% or higher, even if it's not actively raining when you start. This is the tipping point where carrying a packable shell is worth the ounce of prevention.

At 60%+ probability, Should I Run suggests dialing back to easy effort — wet surfaces and reduced visibility make speed work riskier.

## Post-Rain: Watch for Ice

The most dangerous running conditions often come *after* rain, not during it. If temperatures drop below freezing after a rainy period, black ice forms on sidewalks, bridges, and shaded paths. This is Should I Run's "freeze/thaw" ice detection scenario — it scans the previous 24 hours of weather data to flag exactly this situation.
