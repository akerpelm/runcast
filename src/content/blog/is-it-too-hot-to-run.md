You check the forecast. It says 88°F. Is that too hot to run? The answer depends on more than just temperature — **dew point is the real villain**.

## Temperature Alone Isn't Enough

An 88°F day in Phoenix (dew point 35°F) is a completely different beast than 88°F in Houston (dew point 72°F). The difference is humidity, and specifically how much moisture is in the air preventing your sweat from evaporating. Dew point captures this directly.

## The Temp + Dew Point Method

The running community's most widely used heat assessment is the **Hadley method**: add the air temperature and the dew point together.

| Combined (Temp + Dew Point) | What It Means | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| ≤ 100 | Ideal | Run anything |
| 101–120 | Barely noticeable to slightly warm | Normal training, maybe carry water |
| 121–140 | Noticeably warm to hot | Back off intensity, hydrate well |
| 141–160 | Very hot to brutal | Easy effort only, consider treadmill |
| 161–180 | Dangerous | Easy effort with extreme caution |
| > 180 | Hard running NOT recommended | Treadmill day |

**Example:** 88°F + 72°F dew point = **160**. That's in the "brutal" range — you should slow way down or go inside.

Compare: 88°F + 35°F dew point = **123**. That's "noticeably warm" — manageable with some pace adjustment.

## Heat Index: The NWS Thresholds

The National Weather Service Heat Index combines temperature and relative humidity into a single "feels like" number. For runners, these are the critical levels:

- **Below 90°F heat index:** Good conditions for any workout
- **90–104°F:** Use caution — adjust pace, bring water, avoid peak sun
- **105–124°F:** Easy effort only — consider treadmill
- **125°F+:** Do not run outdoors

Remember: runners breathe 10–20x more air per minute than sedentary people, so heat affects you more than the general population.

## How Much Should You Slow Down?

Heat forces your body to divert blood flow to cooling (skin) instead of propulsion (muscles). The pace cost is real:

| Combined Value | Pace Adjustment |
|---|---|
| 121–130 | 1–2% slower |
| 131–140 | 2–3% slower |
| 141–150 | 3–4.5% slower |
| 151–160 | 4.5–6% slower |
| 161–170 | 6–8% slower |

An 8:00/mile easy pace becomes roughly **8:24–8:29** when the combined value hits 153. Fighting this will only make things worse — your heart rate will spike, perceived effort will skyrocket, and recovery will suffer.

## The Decision Framework

Here's a simple flowchart:

1. **Check the combined value** (temp + dew point). If > 180, stay inside.
2. **Check the heat index.** If ≥ 125°F, stay inside.
3. **If combined is 141–180**, run easy only. No speed work, no long runs.
4. **If combined is 121–140**, adjust pace by the percentages above.
5. **If combined ≤ 120**, you're good — run your planned workout.
